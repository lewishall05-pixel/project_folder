Intensive vs. Extensive: Trends of Lethal Violence by the State and Others in South America, 2018–2024

Lewis Sydney Campbell Hall Assignment 1  —  April 2026

I. Introduction

South America is consistently among the most politically violent regions in the world1. State actors are more than three times more likely to kill in any given violent event than non-state counterparts, yet non-states produce greater volume of violence overall. This asymmetry of violence, recorded across 209,317 ACLED datapoints spanning 2018-2024, highlights fundamental distinctions in the character of South American violence; who 'causes' the violence characterises how violence plays out.

State and state-affiliated forces enact violence with intensity. State-based violent events are more likely to end in fatality than not, with armed clashes and organised military operations/responses producing proportionally high death tolls for the number of events involved in. Non-state groups, namely gangs, organised crime and independent militia, comparatively enact violence with extensivity; a far higher volume of recorded violence is non-state based, but fatalities number fewer than one in five, with targeted attacks preceding state-style large confrontations.

The intensive-extensive dynamic holds across the region, albeit not uniformly - Venezuela exhibits particularly high intensity, with a ratio of state to non-state lethality far higher than others consistent with intense government repression. Ecuador presents an anomaly, inverting the regional pattern as gang warfare and prison violence makes it the only nation where non-state lethality exceeds state lethality. Brazil, the continents largest contributor of violence by volume, holds near-parity between actor lethality (see Figure 7).

The following analysis examines how these and other nations in the region produce the intensive-extensive distinction, doing so in spatial, temporal and then mechanical analysis of violence, of the cause of violence, between 2018 and 2024.

II. Data and Methodology

Data is sourced from the Armed Conflict Location and Event Data Project (ACLED), a record of disaggregated political violence and protest events worldwide, coding dates, locations, actor types, event categories, and fatalities for each incident2. The data harvested covers South America from 1st January 2018 (the beginning of data backdating3) to April 2025, although 2025 events had to be removed given an incomplete yearly dataset. After the exclusion of 2025 and filtering out of state nor non-state actors, 209,317 datapoints (events) remained for analysis.

Events were tagged as state or non-state-based events through keyword matching against ACLED actor name fields, producing binary flags (allowing events involving both actors to hold both tags). State actors include those tagged government, state, military or police; non-state actors include those tagged rebel, militia, criminal organisation, gang, rioter, protestor and/or civilian. Fatality figures are compiled from publicly available sources such as newspapers, so whilst wide-reaching, must be understood as lower bounds that may not possess the full picture for rural areas in particular 4.

The primary comparative metric utilised is the lethality ratio, produced through dividing mean state-based fatalities per event by mean non-state-based fatalities per event in a given period/nation. Given right-skewed distribution of per-event fatality - i.e. the top 1% of events accounting for 13% of total fatalities - median figures are reported throughout, and robustness checks excluding the top 1% were carried out for (and confirmed) each key finding 5.

III. Overall Trends in Political Violence

Political violence across South America has remained consistent in volume between 2018 and 2024, with approx. 25,000-32,000 events recorded annually most years6. Fatalities, however, have not - total deaths fluctuate significantly year-on-year, rising sharply through 2022 and 2023 before falling back in a pattern inconsistent with total event count. A year with more recorded political violence is not necessarily a deadlier one, with activity and lethality evidently not correlated with one another, but more so correlated with which actor is more active in a given nation.

Disaggregation by actor type sharpens this picture - non-state armed groups consistently generate the majority of recorded events, and outpace state actors in volume in every year from 2018 to 2024 (see Figure 4). Yet across most of the same period, state actor events produce comparable (/higher) total fatality counts despite a lower number of incidents. In 2021, state actors were responsible for more recorded deaths than non-state actors, despite accounting for fewer than 30% of all events that year in clear demonstration of the intensive/extensive asymmetry in the dataset.

The pattern is not solely an artefact of a small number of catastrophic state-linked events inflating totals. As distributional analysis in the methodology section establishes, extreme outlier events do skew toward state actors, but the lethality gap still persists after their exclusion. The split between state and non-state fatality lines reflects genuine structural difference in how each actor type approaches violence; one with measured but intense force, and the other less calculatedly.

Disaggregating by actor type reveals mechanism underpinning difference; non-state armed groups consistently outnumber state groups in tagged events in every year in the dataset, yet state actors largely match or exceed them in total fatalities most years. IV. The Intensive/Extensive Lethality Gap

The difference between state and non-state actors is one of character more so than scale. State-based events result in lethality 55.2% of the time; more than three times the 17.2% lethality rate of non-state actors. The large majority of non-state violence does not kill - a finding unchanged by removing extreme events for robustness checks, where lethality ratio between the two actor types shifts by just 0.14 after excluding the top 1% of events by fatality count7. The scale of the gap illustrates a systemic difference in the manner each actor operates.

Returning to median fatality count captures this divide most clearly. For state actors, the median event will kill one person, whilst the median across all events (lethal & non-lethal) for non-state actors is zero deaths. lethality ratio, expressing state-based mean fatalities per event as a multiple of the non-state-based equivalent, when calculated stays above 1.0 every year of the dataset, with no exception (see Figure 5). That is, state actors are always more lethal than non-state actors - they are lethal to the degree of consistency that the gap must be structural.

The right-skewed distributional profile of state actors (greater volume of events with greater lethality) established requires a Mann-Whitney U Test to establish statistical significance beyond chance8. Carried out on both the full and robust dataset, the output of a p-value of 0.00 confirms the lethality difference is beyond chance. The null hypothesis for the statement'state and non-state fatality distributions are equal' being rejected confirms the systematic difference, the extensivity, of non-state violence from state.

Key finding State actor events are more than three times as likely to result in at least one fatality compared to non-state actor events (55.2% vs. 17.2%). The state median fatalities per event (1.0) exceeds non-state (0.0), confirming the gap is not driven by outliers. This difference is statistically significant (Mann-Whitney U, p < 0.05) and holds after excluding the top 1% of events by fatality count.

V. Temporal Variation in the Lethality Gap

Whilst the lethality gap is consistent across time, that is not to say its magnitude is static. The gap peaks - i.e. state violence peaks - in 2021 and 2022, before declining across 2023 and 2024; a significant difference to the raw fatality trends, peaking in 2023 numerically. It is therefore important to distinguish that years of high overall violence do not necessarily directly correspond to high state lethality, which is more so depedendent on state-by-state violence aligning compared to generally high non-state violence.

Decomposing the 2021 and 2022 ratio spikes by country and event identifies the nations driving state violence peaks9. Intensification is disproportionately concentrated in Guyana and Suriname, with state lethality rising sharply compared to minimal non-state lethality baselines - a limited number of high-lethality state operations in smaller nations drives the spike, rather than broad regional shift. Paraguay and Argentina by contrast show elevated lethality ratios in baseline and spike periods alike, pointing to structural state intensity rather than the episodic shifts of the former.

Breaking spike years down mechanically (into event type) reinforces this reading, with battles the most lethal category per event, and with battles (armed conflict) exclusively state-based during spike years. Violence against civilians accounts for around 3/4 of non-state-based fatalities in the same period, whilst protests record none. As such, spikes of state intensity are a story of organised armed operations rather than heightened repression of civilians/protest movements; violence and lethality is not always representative of repression.

VI. Spatial Variation Across South America

The intensive/extensive pattern holds across the continent, but with meaningful country-level variation that complicates any proper regional generalisation. Venezuela records the highest state lethality ratio in the dataset (state actors far more lethal per event than non-state), consistent with documenteds pattern of organised repression by Maduro-led state security forces10. Colombia and Peru follow similar directional patterns, reflecting continued activity of military forces against armed groups where state operations are larger-scale and more lethally armed, so are consequently more lethal than non-state counterparts.

Ecuador is an outright exception to the continental dynamic, with non-state fatalities per event far exceeding state fatalities per event per esclating gang warfare and prison violence11. The nation's rapid deterioration into among the most violent in the region is visible in the heatmap as a structural departure from the general intensive/extensive dynamic elsewhere (see Figure 7). Brazil approaches near-parity between actor types, reflecting a context of criminal organisations operating at a scale of armament/organisational complexity comparable to state forces, and therefore broadly equal in lethality.

Suriname, Guyana, and French Guiana show elevated or anomalous ratios in some periods, but do carry significantly lower event counts than larger regional powers. Lethality ratios for these countries are more sensitive to individual incidents and ought to be interpreted with proportionally greater caution than figures for Brazil, Colombia, or Venezuela.

VII. The Mechanism of State Intensity

The final analytical question is not whether state actors kill more intensively - that is established - but more so why this is the case. Event and sub-event type breakdowns point to a structural answer; state fatalities are concentrated in battles and armed clashes, overall organised, often lengthy conflict between security forces and armed opponents that expectedly produce higher death tolls12. Non-state fatalities concentrate almost entirely in attacks - more targeted, brief incidents against specific individuals (or in specific locations) that tend to produce lower death tolls. Both actor types are engaged in fundamentally different forms of conflict from one another, as opposed to carrying out similar violence with simply different levels of force.

This distinction is reinforced by decomposing volume of sub-events for each actor type (see Figure 8). Armed clashes dominate state fatalities, accounting for approximately 2/3 of all deaths in state-based events, with attacks accounting for the remaining third. For non-state actors, the picture is even more concentrated - attacks represent an overwhelming majority of fatalities, with armed clashes a minor secondary contributor. State actors' access to organised combined-arms military operations (with the logistical capacity/firepower said operations entail) explains the per-event lethality advantage more than any other given factor.

It is worth noting that excessive force against protesters (a category that might be expected to be more prominent given protest repression in Venezuela and Bolivia during the period) does not appear as a primary driver of state lethality in the sub-event data 13. State intensity, at least in the South American context of this dataset, is certainly more armed conflict than civilian repression, meaning that regional/international policy response to continental violence requires proper understanding of non-state actors' role in state actors' activity.

VIII. Conclusion

Evidence across the ACLED events recorded in South America between 2018 and 2024 indeed point to the consistent (& statistically robust) conclusion throughout this report that state and non-state actors produce violence in fundamentally different characters. State actors kill intensively, concentrating fatalities into a smaller number of organised, high-lethality operations. Non-state actors kill extensively, generating a higher volume of incidents at substantially lower per-event death tolls. This intensive/extensive distinction holds across every year in the observed period, survives the exclusion of extreme outlier events, and is confirmed as statistically significant by non-parametric testing on both the full and robust datasets 14.

Spatial analysis contributes nuance, but does not undermine, this finding. Venezuela's position at the extreme end of the state intensity spectrum reflects specific domestic contexts of authoritarianism and state willingness to deploy lethal force. Brazil's near-parity adds a dimension, highlighting that the state lethality ratio may be evened if non-state actors are equally organised and armed. Ecuador even entirely inverts the pattern, an anomaly wherein criminal organisations exceed state forces in armament, and consequently in lethality - the state is only more lethal due to material advantage. Altogether these cases highlight the intensive/extensive dynamic is not a fixed property of actor types abstractedly, but a product of specific political-institutional contexts shaping what state and non-state actors are capable of doing and willing to do to achieve it.

Mechanistic findings confirm this - dtate intensity in South America is driven by armed clashes and organised military operations, not by civilian repression or protest crackdowns that tend to dominate international coverage of violence in the region. Frameworks designed to constrain disproportionate force in protest do address a real issue, but target what is evidently a secondary driver of state lethality. The primary driver (organised armed conflict between government forces and non-state armed groups) requires solutions based in ceasefire mechanisms and negotiation of armed groups' dearmament prior to those focused on civilians.

Two limitations do bear restating as conditions on conclusions. One, that actor classification methodology used here relies on keyword matching against name fields, and two, that fatality figures represent a lower bound subject to undercounting biases noted in Section II. A natural extension of this analysis would re-run the intensive/extensive comparison using more extensive actor coding, and would perhaps also find a wider-ranging dataset covering prior to 2018. Further study may extrapolate the intensive/extensive dynamic into regime type or analysis of the military industrial complex, and test the deeper structure of the relationship between state lethality and domestic political context15. The present analysis establishes the pattern; future analysis ought to empiricise it.
